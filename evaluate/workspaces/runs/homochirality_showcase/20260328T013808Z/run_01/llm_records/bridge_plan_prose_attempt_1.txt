Wait — the judge's concern is more fundamental. Let me think about this carefully.

Consider one cycle:
- Start: N_L free L-monomers, N_D free D-monomers, ee = (N_L - N_D)/(N_L + N_D) = ε > 0
- Dry phase: oligomerization occurs. Homochiral oligomers (all-L, all-D) form preferentially (F > 1). Also heterochiral oligomers form.
- Selection: homochiral oligomers survive better (S > 1). Heterochiral oligomers hydrolyze, returning monomers.
- The enrichment mechanism: all-L oligomers sequester L-monomers. All-D oligomers sequester D-monomers. But there are more all-L oligomers (because ee > 0 and the all-L rate ~ [L]^n). So more L is sequestered in stable oligomers than D. The hydrolyzed heterochiral oligomers return a near-racemic mix. So the FREE monomer pool actually gets slightly more D-rich... but the OLIGOMER pool is strongly L-rich. The question is: what happens next cycle? If next cycle's oligomerization draws from the free pool, ee of free pool went DOWN. This is the recycling problem.

The resolution: you have to track the TOTAL system. The surviving homochiral oligomers from cycle 1 persist into cycle 2. They can serve as templates or simply accumulate. If we track ee of the entire system (free + oligomeric), it stays constant (mass conservation). But if we track ee of the *surviving oligomer population* specifically, it grows.

The actual mechanism that works is: **selective destruction of the minority enantiomer's oligomers**. Since there are fewer all-D oligomers than all-L oligomers, and heterochiral oligomers are destroyed, each cycle removes more D (in heterochiral oligomers) than L from the oligomer pool. But wait — heterochiral oligomers contain both L and D roughly equally.

I think the correct framing is: the ratchet works not by enriching the monomer pool directly, but by **selectively accumulating homochiral oligomers** across cycles, with the oligomer population becoming increasingly dominated by the majority enantiomer. The monomer pool may actually decrease over time as material gets locked into stable homochiral oligomers.

This is actually closer to a **Viedma-ripening analog for oligomers**: the homochiral oligomers of the majority enantiomer accumulate, while heterochiral and minority-homochiral oligomers get recycled.
